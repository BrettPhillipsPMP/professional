Program Management & Trial Operations

My main role is to make sure the Blood Research Center’s clinical trials are selected, staffed, and run in a way that we can actually sustain. That means saying yes to the right projects, no to the wrong ones, and being honest about the real cost of “free” work.

  • Portfolio & feasibility
    Industry-sponsored & investigator-initiated hematology studies
    Intake, high-level feasibility, and realistic projections for coordinator time and clinic impact. I care a lot about modeling sponsor delays, early terminations, and the cost of preventable rework.
  • Budget development & negotiation
    Full-cost recovery, CRO intermediation, and escalation plans
    I work directly with sponsors, CROs, and central offices to push for budgets that reflect actual site work. That includes startup fees, subject stipends, protocol amendment fees, and closure costs.
  • Startup & execution
    IRB, contracts, IDS, clinic readiness, SiteVault, CTMS
    I map and refine startup workflows, from IRB and contract timelines to clinic readiness and internal manuals of procedures. I want coordinators to know who owns what and when.
Startup visual mapping Developed detailed visual workflows for industry trials, including parallel IRB/budget streams and internal thresholds for declining studies when budgets do not meet minimum viability.
Data & invoicing oversight Partners with CTMS teams to ensure visit tracking, invoicing triggers, and payment follow-up are reliable, with a focus on avoiding lost revenue due to process gaps.
Preventable rework Tracks time spent fixing avoidable problems (e.g., unclear expectations, incomplete startup) as a distinct category to inform conversations about workload and process design.

Workforce Development & Students

A significant part of my work is building student-powered clinical research teams. At UNC, that has meant creating a structured internship program that supports coordinators while giving students real project experience and a realistic view of clinical research careers.

Student internship programs

  • Multi-year internship pipeline connecting UNC undergrads and community college students with hematology trials.
  • Emphasis on clear expectations, contracts, and structured onboarding.
  • Students support registries, consent workflows, data entry, and startup tasks under coordinator supervision.

Coordinator growth

  • Strategic delegation so coordinators move toward higher-skill work rather than hoarding tasks.
  • Use of interns to develop leadership, mentoring, and project management skills in coordinators.
  • Focus on simple, transferable workflows that students can realistically execute.

I’m also collaborating with institutional leaders on a broader vision: a role focused on connecting students to clinical research opportunities across the School of Medicine, not just within one program. The long-term goal is straightforward: every clinical research project at UNC has meaningful student involvement.

Speaking & Education

I speak and teach on practical clinical research operations, especially for coordinators and early-career professionals. The themes are consistent: clarity, realism, and honest discussion about what it takes to keep a program running without burning people out.

  • Coordinator education in benign hematology – Building a year-long, disease-focused curriculum for coordinators and assistants.
  • History and ethics of informed consent – Lectures for UNC coordinators, blending history with day-to-day practice.
  • Managing a clinical research program – Presentations on portfolio management, budget strategy, and team structure at large academic centers.
  • Certification panels – Discussions on CCRP/CCRC certification, preparation, and career impact.

How I work

My bias is toward clarity, documented expectations, and honest tradeoffs. I’d rather acknowledge constraints openly than pretend a team can do more than it reasonably can.

  • Design workflows that can be taught to a new coordinator or student without guesswork.
  • Track preventable rework and use it as data to improve processes.
  • Align project selection with a clear program vision, not just whoever asks the loudest.
  • Use budgets as a tool for program health, not just a sponsor formality.
